Using LUKS? Good, but watch out.

Posted on March 12, 2016

Do you encrypt your disks/thumbdrives with LUKS? Here’s three things to remember for setting up new volumes:

  • Turn of power management.
  • Prefer a faster over a harder hash function (but always use a secure one).
  • Think twice on what you put inside.

There are a few more, but the above three were the points that – for me – stood out most when I had a quick glance at this nice paper by Simone Bossi and Andrea Visconti.

They pick on a few issues with key-derivation in LUKS. Basically, LUKS tries to find a sweet spot between security and usability when parameters are selected in the setup process for a new LUKS-encrypted volume. That means the number of Hashing-Rounds for Key-Derivation is derived from the current performance of your machine. (LUKS, from v.1.7.0 on, aims for 2 seconds of runtime for key-derivation.) So when your computer (especially your laptop) is in power saving mode and/or under heavy load, you get a lot less rounds than in full power mode. Also, because harder hash functions tend to be slower, the number of rounds may also be significantly reduced.

Third, if a known plaintext is available (the paper uses an ext-fs header as example), one gets a cheap key-distinguisher compared to the built-in hash-distinguisher, speeding up a brute-force attempt by about 25%.

Do I need to worry?

Depends. If you are fighting the NSA, probably. If you are hiding the list of already ordered christmas presents (only 287 days left!) from you spouse, you’re still fine.

Bootnote

There’s another paper by the same authors showing that a brute force-attack can be made even faster by exploiting some properties of PBKDF2.
